Carb service (+ lean stall)

not started

Status: not started — needs carb pull and service

Symptoms

  • Stalls at idle when leaned over — on either side, even fully warm. Idles fine when vertical. Dies slowly (not instant kill), throttle keeps it alive.
  • General carb condition unknown — bike sat unused for an unknown period before purchase. Carbs may have varnish or partial clogging from stale fuel.

Diagnosis

The slow die-off on either side confirms fuel starvation, not electrical — the sidestand switch would be an instant kill and only triggers on the left. At lean angles, fuel shifts away from the pilot jets. Dirty or partially blocked pilots make the idle circuit marginal — upright it barely works, leaned over it can't sustain combustion.

Service procedure

  1. Remove carbs (loosen airbox clamps and intake boots, disconnect throttle/choke cables, fuel lines, drain bowls)
  2. Disassemble bowls. Note mixture screw turns-out before removal (do not force)
  3. Clean all jets — especially pilot jets (#38). Use carb cleaner and compressed air through all passages
  4. Inspect float height, needle valve seats, diaphragms for tears
  5. Check air filter — replace if degraded (due every 18,000 km per Haynes schedule)
  6. Reinstall, sync carbs with vacuum gauge (max 20mm Hg difference between cylinders)
  7. Set idle to 1200 rpm

Context

Haynes maintenance schedule calls for carb sync every 12,000 km. At ~72,000 km with no documented carb work, this is long overdue. The prior owner's parts list shows piston rings and clutch kit replaced (2024–2025), suggesting the engine was apart, but no mention of carb service.

Front brake fluid low

needs attention

Status: not started — should do before ARK inspection

Observed: front reservoir window shows no visible fluid. Brakes still feel fine.

Likely cause

Normal pad wear is the most common reason. As pads thin, caliper pistons extend further, drawing fluid from the reservoir. If pads are at or near minimum thickness, the reservoir will appear empty. This is by design — it's telling you to check the pads.

Less likely but check

  • Leak at master cylinder, banjo bolt, or caliper — look for wet spots along the brake line. Per Haynes: "if either fluid reservoir requires repeated topping-up there could be a hydraulic leak which must be investigated immediately."
  • Caliper slide pin seizure — common on winter-ridden Transalps. Causes uneven pad wear and binding. Check both pins slide freely, clean and grease with copperslip during reassembly.

Procedure (Haynes 0-18)

  1. Support bike upright. Turn handlebars so reservoir is level
  2. Level must be above LOWER line in window
  3. If below: undo 2 reservoir cover screws, remove cover, diaphragm plate, and diaphragm
  4. Top up with DOT 4 to the ridge on the inside front wall of the reservoir. Do not overfill
  5. Ensure diaphragm is correctly seated before reinstalling cover
  6. Check pad thickness — Haynes Section 3 for wear limits
  7. If brakes feel spongy after topping up → air in system, bleed required (Haynes Chapter 7)

Brake fluid change interval

Haynes schedule: change every 2 years or 18,000 km. With unknown service history, the fluid is almost certainly old. Old DOT 4 absorbs moisture (hygroscopic), which lowers boiling point and causes internal corrosion. Recommendation: do a full fluid change, not just a top-up. This means bleeding through fresh fluid at all calipers.

Warning

DOT 4 damages painted surfaces. Wrap a rag around the reservoir when working. Do not use fluid from an already-opened container that's been sitting — it will have absorbed moisture.

Headlight aim + steering lock blocked

needs solution

Status: investigated — ignition barrel relocation needed

Observed (2026-04-23): headlight aims too high, blinding following riders at night. Cannot turn key to steering lock position.

What was tried

Removed front cowling to adjust headlight downward. Two clearance conflicts found:

  1. Ignition barrel blocks headlight rotation — the barrel body physically prevents tilting the Flashpoint LED 7" headlight further down
  2. KOSO speedo blocks steering lock — the speedo body prevents the key from turning to the lock position

Both trace to the ignition barrel position being incompatible with the aftermarket headlight bracket and KOSO mount. Relocating the barrel fixes both.

HISS immobilizer constraint

The XL650V uses Honda's HISS (Honda Ignition Security System). The HISS antenna ring sits around the ignition barrel and reads the transponder chip in the key. Per xrv.org.uk: the antenna ring must stay physically around the barrel — it cannot be separated. When relocating, the barrel + HISS ring move together as one unit.

Relocation approach

  • No off-the-shelf relocation kits exist for the Transalp. Need a custom bracket.
  • Possible mounting points: side of the top yoke, frame tube below the tank, or a panel-mount solution
  • The wiring harness needs enough slack to reach the new position — check loom length before committing to a location
  • The steering lock mechanism is built into the frame at the stock barrel location — relocating the barrel means losing the steering lock entirely (acceptable tradeoff for a custom build, but consider a disc lock instead)

ARK consideration

Headlight aim that's too high could fail the ARK roadworthiness inspection. This should be resolved before the 05/2026 deadline. Even a temporary fix (shimming the headlight bracket) may be worth doing if the barrel relocation takes time.

Oil on bash plate (left side)

not started

Status: not started — needs clean-and-ride test

Observed: oil residue on the left side of the bash plate.

Most common sources (XL650V forums)

  1. Valve cover (rocker cover) gasket — most frequently reported oil leak on this engine. Oil trickles down the cylinder and collects below. Gasket hardens with age and heat cycles.
  2. Starter motor O-ring — single O-ring at crankcase/starter interface on the left side. Can weep under vibration or high revs.
  3. Oil drain plug washer — reused or compressed crush washer. Drain plug torque: 30 Nm (Haynes).
  4. Chain lube sling-off — benign, but can look like a leak.

Diagnostic procedure

  1. Check oil level first — dipstick on right-side crankcase cover, rest on threads (do not screw in)
  2. Clean the entire left side of the engine and bash plate thoroughly with degreaser
  3. Ride for 30–50 km
  4. Inspect: trace the fresh oil trail from highest wet point downward. Highest point = source
  5. If valve cover gasket: replace gasket, clean mating surfaces
  6. If starter O-ring: pull starter motor, replace O-ring

Per Haynes: "If you have to add oil frequently, check whether you have any oil leaks from the engine joints, seals and gaskets."

Tach over-read

investigating

Status: investigating — next step: try pulse=2

Observed (2026-04-08): tach reads ~1.5x higher than reality.

ConditionKoso showsLikely actualFactor
Warm idle2,000 RPM~1,300 RPM~1.54
Cold idle (choke on)3,000 RPM~1,900 RPM~1.58
Full revspegs 10k+~8,500 redline~1.5

Note: Haynes confirms XL650V idle speed is 1200 ± 100 rpm, which aligns with the "likely actual" column above.

Confirmed correct settings

  • Pulse = 1 (book value for 4C-2P, 4-stroke V-twin)
  • Polarity = Hi
  • Bar range = 10,000 RPM

What was tried

Changed pulse from 1 → 1.5. Verified the setting saved (re-entered settings menu, saw 1.5). Idle reading did not change from 2,000 RPM. This rules out a simple pulse-multiplier error — if pulse values affected the display linearly, going from 1 to 1.5 should have dropped the reading by ~33%. It didn't move at all.

Interpretation

The Koso may be treating 1.5 as a no-op (no engine type mapped to it in the manual's table), or the tach isn't deriving its reading from the actual coil pulse frequency at all. Either way, the fix is not likely a software-only setting tweak.

Next steps to try, in order

  1. Pulse = 2. This corresponds to a valid engine type (4C-4P) and should definitely halve the reading if the setting does anything. If idle drops from 2,000 to ~1,000 → the setting system works but the correct value is somewhere else (try 1.5 again, or accept the 2x and note the scale). If idle still shows 2,000 → the Koso isn't reading pulse count, and the fix is hardware.
  2. Polarity Lo. Toggle step 6 from Hi → Lo and see if reading changes at all. If it goes to 0, wrong polarity. If it goes erratic, signal noise.
  3. Confirm spark plugs are R-type. XL650V OEM is NGK DPR8EA-9 — the "R" is the resistor. Non-resistor plugs cause RFI that the Koso tach circuit picks up as extra pulses. The Koso manual explicitly calls this out. Pull a plug and check the part number.
  4. Trace the RPM wire back to its tap. The book assumes the tap is on one coil + pin. If this install is on a shared +12V bus feeding both coils, the Koso sees both coils' pulses and over-reads. Fix = move the tap to the + pin of a single coil.
  5. Check for noise pickup. If the RPM wire runs near HT leads or the coil secondaries, capacitive coupling can inject extra pulses. Reroute or shield.
  6. Verify real idle with an independent source — a timing-light tach or the front cylinder's coil output on a scope — to confirm what the actual idle RPM is and how far off the Koso really is.

Fuel gauge lean-over dip

understood

Status: understood — no fix needed, use Trip A as primary fuel gauge

Observed: the fuel gauge drops to empty for a while when the bike is leaned over (cornering or on the side stand).

Cause

Physical, not electrical. The Honda fuel sender in the tank is a float-on-arm resistive sensor. When the bike leans, fuel sloshes to the low side of the tank. If the sender sits on the high side, the float drops into an empty pocket and the sender reports empty. The Koso is faithfully displaying what the sender sends. Nothing is wrong with the dash or the sender.

Workaround — use Trip A as the primary fuel gauge

  • Reset Trip A at every fill-up: on the main screen, press Adjust once to see Trip A, then hold Adjust 3s to reset.
  • Tank capacity: 19.6 L. Consumption: 4.2–5.2 L/100km. Range: 280–350 km.
  • Rule of thumb: refuel by 250 km and you always have reserve.
  • Leave Trip B untouched as a trip/week accumulator.
  • Treat the fuel bars as a secondary sanity check, not a source of truth.

Not the same issue as lean stall — that's actual fuel starvation at the pilot jets at extreme lean angle (a carburetor problem). Both involve fuel slosh in a tilted tank, but the fixes are unrelated: the gauge dip has no fix, the stall does.
