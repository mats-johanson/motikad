Rear cylinder dead — after carb cleaner on stuck pilot screw

investigating — bike not rideable

Status: investigating (2026-04-27). Diagnosis depends on plug inspection. Bike currently runs on one cylinder only.

Observed (2026-04-27)

  • Bike runs on one cylinder only — very little power, needs constant throttle to stay alive.
  • Choke no longer raises RPM. Used to work normally before this.
  • Symptom appeared after several days of spraying carb cleaner into the rear carb's stuck pilot screw (left side of bike) and wiggling the screw to try to free it. Screw still only moves about a quarter turn.

Two main candidate causes (both consistent with the symptoms)

  1. Both rear plugs drowned with carb cleaner. Repeated solvent spray past the screw reaches the rear cylinder via the pilot (idle) circuit. Both plugs in that cylinder get coated, neither fires, cylinder goes dead. The choke can't help because the choke just adds more fuel — and a drowned plug won't ignite the fuel anyway. The XL650V has two plugs per cylinder sharing one combustion chamber, so it takes both being fouled to kill the cylinder — repeated dousing is enough to do that.
  2. The pilot screw's rubber seal (o-ring) is damaged. Carb cleaner attacks NBR rubber. Days of soaking + wiggling can swell or crack the seal. Without an intact seal the screw can no longer control the fuel/air mix at idle — that cylinder runs unmetered (effectively a permanent vacuum leak), too lean to fire. Direct precedent on a different Honda: CBR250 forum thread — single carb's pilot screw with a missing seal dropped only that one cylinder, recurring until the seal was replaced. Haynes 4-13 §21 explicitly lists the o-ring as a wear/damage check item.

A third, less likely possibility: the rear ignition coil (or its HT lead) has died. The XL650V has one coil per cylinder, firing both plugs at once via a Y-shaped lead (Haynes 5-2 §1). A coil failure kills both plugs simultaneously and looks like a dead cylinder. Easy to rule out with a multimeter coil test.

Why this happened

The stuck-pilot-screw procedure documents that carb cleaner is the wrong tool here — the right approach is a real penetrating oil (50/50 power steering fluid + acetone, or Kroil, or PB Blaster), then heat, then re-cutting the screw slot, then pulling the carb. The conversational guidance that drove the days-of-spraying approach contradicted the documented procedure. Both the procedure and its "do not use" list have been tightened so this advice can't repeat.

Diagnostic next step — pull rear cylinder LEFT plug

Of the four plugs on this bike, the rear cylinder's left-hand plug is the only one accessible without propping the tank (Haynes 1-11 §5.3 — "easy and unrestricted"). Both plugs in the rear cylinder share the same combustion chamber, so whichever cause is at work, it should show on this easy-access plug.

Read the plug using the plug-reading guide:

What the plug looks likeWhat it tells youNext step
Wet, black, smells of carb cleaner / solvent Drowning theory holds. Cylinder is fouled, not broken. Pull rear right plug too (needs tank prop) to confirm both look the same. Clean or replace both plugs. Stop spraying carb cleaner. Then re-test the bike.
White / chalky / very clean and dry Lean misfire. Strongly points at a damaged pilot-screw rubber seal letting unmetered air in. Pull the pilot screw assembly from the rear carb. Replace the o-ring (and ideally the whole 4-piece assembly) — Hippotech XL650V kit (~€10–20 on Amazon, includes 2 of each part).

Confirm which cylinder is actually dead with a "pull-cap" test: with engine running, pull each plug cap one at a time. The cap on the dead cylinder won't change engine note when removed. Both caps on a live cylinder need to be removed to kill it.

What to stop doing immediately

  • Stop spraying carb cleaner at the stuck pilot screw. It's not freeing the screw and it's the suspected cause of the new symptoms.
  • Don't run the bike longer than necessary on one cylinder. The live (front) cylinder is doing 100% of the work that was being shared — running hotter and harder than designed. Meanwhile the dead cylinder pumps unburned fuel into the crankcase past the piston rings ("fuel wash"), thinning the oil and reducing its ability to lubricate. On a 23-year-old engine you don't want to accelerate that. Porikärbes has no catalytic converter (Arrow direct-through, see parts), so the cat-cooking risk that applies to a stock XL650V isn't a factor here. Riding it as transport is still a no — half power, won't idle, not safe in traffic. Brief idling for diagnosis (under a minute or two) is fine.

Parts likely needed (whichever branch wins)

  • Hippotech XL650V pilot-screw kit (Amazon, ~€10–20) — includes 2× of: pilot screw, spring, washer, o-ring. Cheap insurance, fits both carbs. Order now even if the diagnosis is "drowning" — the o-ring on the rear carb has had several days of solvent exposure and is suspect anyway.
  • Spare NGK DPR8EA-9 spark plugs — if the drowning branch wins, easier to swap than to clean. Buy 4 (whole set) since they're cheap.
  • If pulling the carb: Keyster XL650V '00–06 carb kit (nrp-carbs.co.uk) — full set of jets, gaskets, o-rings.
  • 50/50 power steering fluid + acetone (or Kroil, or PB Blaster) — for the stuck-screw work, replacing the carb cleaner approach.

Left lean stall / carb service

not started

Status: on hold pending rear cylinder dead diagnosis (2026-04-27). Step 0 (mixture screw test) was attempted in-situ but the rear carb's pilot screw seized after a partial turn; the in-situ recovery approach (carb cleaner flushing) caused the rear cylinder to drop. Continue here only after the rear-cylinder-dead issue is resolved.

Symptoms

  • Stalls at idle only when leaned LEFT — right lean is fine, no RPM dip. Fast left lean dies quickly. Upright idle holds but feels marginal.
  • Throttle keeps it alive even when leaned left — giving gas sustains the engine regardless of lean angle.
  • Previous owner's note: admitted he may have set the mixture screws too lean during a past idle adjustment — "Maybe I once turned it too tight while adjusting idle."

Diagnosis

Points to the left carburetor's pilot (idle) circuit being marginal — either set too lean or partially blocked:

  • Left-only stall rules out symmetric problems (both pilots equally dirty, tank pickup, fuel line). When leaned left, fuel shifts in the left carb's bowl away from its pilot jet pickup. Left cylinder drops out, and a V-twin at idle can't sustain on one cylinder alone — it dies.
  • Throttle keeps it alive rules out electrical cuts. The sidestand switch would be an instant kill regardless of RPM. Above idle, the main jet circuit takes over and bypasses the marginal pilot, so the left cylinder still gets fuel when you give gas.
  • Petcock ruled out. Vacuum petcock is on the left underside of the tank — leaning left increases hydrostatic head above it, so flow would increase, not decrease. Plus tank is full, so pickup is submerged at any angle.

Clogged pilot jets are "the single most common carburetor problem" (Randakk / Ari Henning). But the PO's own note suggests the screws may just be set too lean — a 5-minute fix with a screwdriver.

Step 0 — Mixture screw test (do this before anything else)

If enrichening the pilot mixture fixes the stall, the circuits are clean and no disassembly is needed. Free test, ~30 minutes including tank prop.

  1. Prop the tank for access to the carbs.
  2. Warm the engine fully — mixture only means anything at operating temp.
  3. Confirm idle is at 1200 ± 100 rpm.
  4. Follow the pilot screw tuning procedure. For this stall, Strategy C (symmetric enrich) is the right starting point — the suspect is the rear (left cylinder) carb's pilot circuit, but keeping both carbs balanced preserves sync.
  5. Rear carb pilot screw is on the left side of the carb bank (suspect side). Front carb on the right.
  6. Log final turn-out values per carb in history.html.
  7. No change after ½ turn past PO baseline on both → proceed to full carb service below.

If a pilot screw is stuck — STOP, read this first

If either pilot screw will only turn part of a revolution (a "stuck" or "seized" screw), do not try to free it with carb cleaner, brake cleaner, or repeated wiggling. Both solvents attack the screw's NBR rubber seal — the o-ring inside the carb (Haynes 4-13 §21). Days of in-situ flushing destroy the seal even if the screw eventually moves, and on a twin-plug head like the XL650V the failure mode is a dead cylinder with no warning fouled-plug stage. This already happened once on Porikärbes — see rear cylinder dead (2026-04-27). Use the stuck-pilot-screw ladder in the procedures page: real penetrating oil (50/50 power steering fluid + acetone, Kroil, or PB Blaster), then heat, then re-cut the slot, then pull the carb. Hard limit: two soak-cycle rounds with no progress = escalate, don't repeat.

Service procedure (if Step 0 fails)

  1. Remove both carbs (loosen airbox clamps and intake boots, disconnect throttle/choke cables, fuel lines, drain bowls)
  2. Disassemble bowls. Record final Step 0 mixture screw positions before removal.
  3. Clean all jets (Estonian: žiklöörid) — focus on the left carb's pilot jet (#38) and its air/fuel passages. Carb cleaner + compressed air through every passage, not just the jet.
  4. Measure float height against Haynes spec (7 mm). Lay carb on its side, float resting lightly on valve without compressing the spring, measure from gasket face to top of float. Known Transalp issue: some measured at 15 mm (too low → low bowl fuel → lean-angle sensitive). Adjust by bending the float tang if needed.
  5. Inspect needle valve seats and CV diaphragms for tears — replace any that look suspect
  6. Check air filter — replace if degraded (due every 18,000 km per Haynes schedule)
  7. Reinstall, sync carbs with vacuum gauge (≤ 20mm Hg difference between cylinders)
  8. Set idle to 1200 rpm, final mixture screw fine-tune

Parts needed (only if pulling carbs)

  • Carb cleaner aerosol (with straw nozzle for jet passages)
  • Air filter (inspect first, replace if degraded)
  • Possibly: CV diaphragms (not in every Keyster kit — inspect first)

Front brake fluid low

needs attention

Status: not started — should do before ARK inspection

Observed: front reservoir window shows no visible fluid. Brakes still feel fine.

Likely cause

Normal pad wear is the most common reason. As pads thin, caliper pistons extend further, drawing fluid from the reservoir. If pads are at or near minimum thickness, the reservoir will appear empty. This is by design — it's telling you to check the pads.

Less likely but check

  • Leak at master cylinder, banjo bolt, or caliper — look for wet spots along the brake line. Per Haynes: "if either fluid reservoir requires repeated topping-up there could be a hydraulic leak which must be investigated immediately."
  • Caliper slide pin seizure — common on winter-ridden Transalps. Causes uneven pad wear and binding. Check both pins slide freely, clean and grease with copperslip during reassembly.

Procedure (Haynes 0-18)

  1. Support bike upright. Turn handlebars so reservoir is level
  2. Level must be above LOWER line in window
  3. If below: undo 2 reservoir cover screws, remove cover, diaphragm plate, and diaphragm
  4. Top up with DOT 4 to the ridge on the inside front wall of the reservoir. Do not overfill
  5. Ensure diaphragm is correctly seated before reinstalling cover
  6. Check pad thickness — Haynes Section 3 for wear limits
  7. If brakes feel spongy after topping up → air in system, bleed required (Haynes Chapter 7)

Brake fluid change interval

Haynes schedule: change every 2 years or 18,000 km. With unknown service history, the fluid is almost certainly old. Old DOT 4 absorbs moisture (hygroscopic), which lowers boiling point and causes internal corrosion. Recommendation: do a full fluid change, not just a top-up. This means bleeding through fresh fluid at all calipers.

Warning

DOT 4 damages painted surfaces. Wrap a rag around the reservoir when working. Do not use fluid from an already-opened container that's been sitting — it will have absorbed moisture.

Headlight aim + steering lock blocked

needs solution

Status: investigated — ignition barrel relocation needed

Observed (2026-04-23): headlight aims too high, blinding following riders at night. Cannot turn key to steering lock position.

What was tried

Removed front cowling to adjust headlight downward. Two clearance conflicts found:

  1. Ignition barrel blocks headlight rotation — the barrel body physically prevents tilting the Flashpoint LED 7" headlight further down
  2. KOSO speedo blocks steering lock — the speedo body prevents the key from turning to the lock position

Both trace to the ignition barrel position being incompatible with the aftermarket headlight bracket and KOSO mount. Relocating the barrel fixes both.

HISS immobilizer constraint

The XL650V uses Honda's HISS (Honda Ignition Security System). The HISS antenna ring sits around the ignition barrel and reads the transponder chip in the key. Per xrv.org.uk: the antenna ring must stay physically around the barrel — it cannot be separated. When relocating, the barrel + HISS ring move together as one unit.

Relocation approach

  • No off-the-shelf relocation kits exist for the Transalp. Need a custom bracket.
  • Possible mounting points: side of the top yoke, frame tube below the tank, or a panel-mount solution
  • The wiring harness needs enough slack to reach the new position — check loom length before committing to a location
  • The steering lock mechanism is built into the frame at the stock barrel location — relocating the barrel means losing the steering lock entirely (acceptable tradeoff for a custom build, but consider a disc lock instead)

ARK consideration

Headlight aim that's too high could fail the ARK roadworthiness inspection. This should be resolved before the 05/2026 deadline. Even a temporary fix (shimming the headlight bracket) may be worth doing if the barrel relocation takes time.

Left switchgear — flaky turn signal + stuck high beam

resolved

Status: resolved 2026-04-25 — electrical contact cleaner flush fixed both symptoms immediately, no disassembly required. Diagnosis confirmed: oxidised contacts + gummed detents, not broken parts. Entry kept for reference if symptoms return.

Observed

  • Turn signal cancel button cancels on a light touch (no full press needed). Sometimes doesn't activate the indicator at all. Sometimes flickers when held.
  • High beam button gets stuck in the pressed-in position and doesn't pop out on its own.

Likely cause

Two failure modes inside one 23-year-old switch cluster:

  • Oxidised / worn contacts on the turn-signal switch. Old copper or brass contacts develop a non-conductive oxide layer; intermittent or no signal until the surfaces wipe each other clean. The "cancels on light touch" symptom suggests the cancel contact is over-eager because the actuator spring is weak or the detent is gummed, letting it return-to-cancel under almost any pressure.
  • Gummed detent / return spring on the high-beam switch. The pop-out mechanism is a small spring + detent inside the housing; old grease or dirt thickens up and the spring can't push the actuator back. Common on every aging Japanese bike.

Fix ladder — gentlest first

  1. In-situ contact cleaner flush. Spray DeoxIT D5 or generic electrical contact cleaner into the switch through the actuator gap and any visible seams. Work each switch 50+ times to scrub the contacts. Let dry. Test. This alone fixes most flaky old switchgear.
  2. Open the cluster. 2 small Phillips screws on the underside of the housing. Half-shells separate. Inside: small contact pads (clean with contact cleaner + cotton swab, light scrub if oxidised), springs (check tension — replace if collapsed), detent balls/pins (apply a tiny amount of dielectric grease, never copper grease). Reassemble carefully — small parts will spring loose if the housing is opened upside-down.
  3. Replace the cluster if contacts are pitted/eroded or a spring is broken. Used OEM XL650V left switch cluster on eBay or forums (~€30–60). Aftermarket universal clusters work but the connector pinout will need to be matched manually.

Tools

  • Small Phillips screwdriver (PH1)
  • Electrical contact cleaner — DeoxIT D5 preferred, brake cleaner is NOT suitable (residue, plastic-incompatible)
  • Cotton swabs
  • Dielectric grease (small tube, ~€5)
  • Tray or magnetic mat to catch tiny springs and detent balls

ARK relevance

Estonian roadworthiness inspection tests all lights including turn signals. A flaky indicator that doesn't activate reliably or flickers is a likely fail. Add to ARK pre-inspection checks; resolve before booking.

Tach over-read

investigating

Status: investigating — next step: try pulse=2

Observed (2026-04-08): tach reads ~1.5x higher than reality.

ConditionKoso showsLikely actualFactor
Warm idle2,000 RPM~1,300 RPM~1.54
Cold idle (choke on)3,000 RPM~1,900 RPM~1.58
Full revspegs 10k+~8,500 redline~1.5

Note: Haynes confirms XL650V idle speed is 1200 ± 100 rpm, which aligns with the "likely actual" column above.

Confirmed correct settings

  • Pulse = 1 (book value for 4C-2P, 4-stroke V-twin)
  • Polarity = Hi
  • Bar range = 10,000 RPM

What was tried

Changed pulse from 1 → 1.5. Verified the setting saved (re-entered settings menu, saw 1.5). Idle reading did not change from 2,000 RPM. This rules out a simple pulse-multiplier error — if pulse values affected the display linearly, going from 1 to 1.5 should have dropped the reading by ~33%. It didn't move at all.

Interpretation

The Koso may be treating 1.5 as a no-op (no engine type mapped to it in the manual's table), or the tach isn't deriving its reading from the actual coil pulse frequency at all. Either way, the fix is not likely a software-only setting tweak.

Next steps to try, in order

  1. Pulse = 2. This corresponds to a valid engine type (4C-4P) and should definitely halve the reading if the setting does anything. If idle drops from 2,000 to ~1,000 → the setting system works but the correct value is somewhere else (try 1.5 again, or accept the 2x and note the scale). If idle still shows 2,000 → the Koso isn't reading pulse count, and the fix is hardware.
  2. Polarity Lo. Toggle step 6 from Hi → Lo and see if reading changes at all. If it goes to 0, wrong polarity. If it goes erratic, signal noise.
  3. Confirm all four spark plugs are R-type. XL650V is a twin-plug head — two plugs per cylinder, four total. OEM is NGK DPR8EA-9: the "R" is the resistor that suppresses radio noise from the spark. A single non-resistor plug anywhere on the bike causes RFI that the KOSO tach circuit picks up as extra pulses. The KOSO manual explicitly calls this out. Pull any plug (rear-left is easiest, no tank work) and check the part number — but verify all four match before ruling this branch in or out.
  4. Trace the RPM wire back to its tap. The book assumes the tap is on one coil + pin. If this install is on a shared +12V bus feeding both coils, the Koso sees both coils' pulses and over-reads. Fix = move the tap to the + pin of a single coil.
  5. Check for noise pickup. If the RPM wire runs near HT leads or the coil secondaries, capacitive coupling can inject extra pulses. Reroute or shield.
  6. Verify real idle with an independent source — a timing-light tach or the front cylinder's coil output on a scope — to confirm what the actual idle RPM is and how far off the Koso really is.

Fuel gauge lean-over dip

understood

Status: understood — no fix needed, use Trip A as primary fuel gauge

Observed: the fuel gauge drops to empty for a while when the bike is leaned over (cornering or on the side stand).

Cause

Physical, not electrical. The Honda fuel sender in the tank is a float-on-arm resistive sensor. When the bike leans, fuel sloshes to the low side of the tank. If the sender sits on the high side, the float drops into an empty pocket and the sender reports empty. The Koso is faithfully displaying what the sender sends. Nothing is wrong with the dash or the sender.

Workaround — use Trip A as the primary fuel gauge

  • Reset Trip A at every fill-up: on the main screen, press Adjust once to see Trip A, then hold Adjust 3s to reset.
  • Tank capacity: 19.6 L. Consumption: 4.2–5.2 L/100km. Range: 280–350 km.
  • Rule of thumb: refuel by 250 km and you always have reserve.
  • Leave Trip B untouched as a trip/week accumulator.
  • Treat the fuel bars as a secondary sanity check, not a source of truth.

Not the same issue as lean stall — that's actual fuel starvation at the pilot jets at extreme lean angle (a carburetor problem). Both involve fuel slosh in a tilted tank, but the fixes are unrelated: the gauge dip has no fix, the stall does.
