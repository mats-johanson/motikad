Fuel tank — prop or full removal

Applies to: XL650V Transalp (2000–2007). Procedure differs from XL600V — this is the vacuum-tap / no-manual-OFF version.

When to prop vs. remove

  • Prop (tilt at rear): work on top of the carbs (sync vacuum takeoffs, intake rubbers), airbox removal, spark plug access. Fastest, no drain. (Mixture screws don't need this — they're side-access, see pilot screw tuning.)
  • Remove fully: frame work, steering head bearings, painting/cleaning the tank, replacing the fuel tap or level sender, wiring access.

Key facts (XL650V)

  • Fuel tap is automatic vacuum-operated — no ON/OFF/RES lever. Closes itself when engine stops (page 4-2).
  • Tap sits under the left side of the tank, with two hoses: fuel out (larger bore, wire-spring clamp with two ears, goes to carbs) and vacuum in (smaller bore, friction fit or tiny plastic clip, from a carb intake). The clamp type is the fastest way to tell them apart.
  • Integral strainer inside the tank — no external fuel filter to service.
  • No tank draining needed for prop or removal. Expect a small dribble from the fuel hose when detached.
  • Tank capacity 19.6 L — full tank is heavy (≈15 kg fuel + tank weight). If full, plan on two hands or an assistant for full removal.
  • Tank mounting bolt torque: no specific spec listed — snug, not torqued like structural fasteners.

Tools

  • 10 mm socket + ratchet (rear mounting bolt)
  • Pliers for spring hose clamps
  • Flat screwdriver (prying clamps if stubborn)
  • Rag + small tray or cup (hose dribble)
  • Block of wood ~15–20 cm (prop only)
  • For full removal: soft surface to rest the tank on (cardboard, towel)

Prop procedure

  1. Fuel cap secure. Confirm engine is off (tap closes automatically).
  2. Seat off. Key-operated lock — on stock Transalp bodywork it's behind the left side panel near the rear; on Porikärbes (custom stripped bodywork) look for a keyhole on the side of the subframe near the rear of the seat. Turn the key, lift the rear of the seat, and slide it back/up off the front retaining tabs.
  3. Place a rag under the fuel tap on the left underside of the tank. Have a small tray or cup ready for the hose dribble.
  4. Detach the fuel hose. Fuel hose = larger bore with the wire-spring clamp (two metal ears sticking out). Grip the two ears with water-pump or needle-nose pliers and squeeze them together — that opens the clamp. Slide the clamp down the hose, away from the spigot. Then twist the hose side-to-side while pulling — do not yank straight, as 23-year-old hose rubber is stiff and will tear before it releases. Expect a small fuel dribble into your rag/tray.
  5. Detach the vacuum hose. Smaller bore, usually a friction fit (or tiny plastic clip — squeeze to release). Pull straight off. No fuel inside, just air.
  6. Unscrew the rear tank mounting bolt (single 10 mm bolt at the rear of the tank, accessible once the seat is off — if you don't see it, check for a rubber plug hiding it). Set the bolt aside where you won't lose it.
  7. Lift the rear of the tank ~15–20 cm slowly. Watch the breather/overflow hoses and the fuel-level sensor wire on the underside — stop lifting the moment any of them goes taut.
  8. If taut: disconnect the fuel-level sensor connector (press the plastic locking tab with a fingernail or small screwdriver before pulling — don't force) and release the breather/overflow hoses from their tank unions. Otherwise leave them connected.
  9. Prop the tank securely. Slide the wood block under the rear of the tank so the block rests on a solid part of the frame/subframe (not on delicate wiring or fairing brackets) and the tank rests on the block. Verify the tank won't fall before reaching underneath — push lightly on the tank to test. If it wobbles or wants to slide, reposition the block.
  10. Work on the engine. To reinstall: reverse the steps.

Full removal

  1. Steps 1–6 above.
  2. Lift the rear higher and disconnect the fuel-level sensor wiring connector, then the overflow and breather hoses from their unions (note routing — photograph before disconnecting if unsure).
  3. Draw the tank back and up off the bike (it slides rearward off front rubber mounts).
  4. Check the tank mounting rubbers (front mounts + any pads) for damage — replace if perished.
  5. Rest the tank on a soft surface, away from heat and sparks.

Reinstall checklist

  • Tank mounting rubbers in place at the front.
  • Breather and overflow hoses routed correctly, not pinched or kinked.
  • Fuel-level sensor connector fully seated.
  • Fuel hose pushed fully onto the tap spigot, spring clamp back in original position over the joint.
  • Vacuum hose pushed fully onto its spigot.
  • Rear mounting bolt snug.
  • Start engine, check for leaks at tap + hose joints before riding.

Gotchas

  • Running the engine with tank propped: impossible without a workaround — fuel line is detached. For tests that need the engine running (e.g. carb sync with vacuum gauges), either rig a gravity-fed aux fuel container (Haynes 1-21 §17 step 9) or drop the tank back down between adjustments.
  • Fuel cap vent: if the cap's vent is blocked, a vacuum builds in the tank and fuel starves. Unrelated to this procedure, but if you get odd starvation symptoms after reassembly, check the cap vent.
  • Spring clamps stretch: if a clamp no longer grips, replace with a worm-drive clamp of the right size. A loose fuel clamp will leak.

Manual reference

Haynes §2 "Fuel tank and tap — removal and installation" — XL650V procedure on page 4-3 (steps 7–13 for removal, step 11 explicitly covers propping with a wood block).

Spark plugs — pull, inspect, replace

Applies to: XL650V Transalp.

The bike has FOUR spark plugs

Each cylinder has two plugs (Haynes 1-11 §5.1 says verbatim: "Note that each cylinder has two spark plugs"). This is called a "twin-plug head". One ignition coil per cylinder fires both of that cylinder's plugs at the same instant, through a Y-shaped high-tension lead (Haynes 5-2 §1). Two flame-fronts burn the fuel/air mix faster and more evenly than one. Total: front cyl R + L plugs, rear cyl R + L plugs = 4 plugs.

Why this matters when you're diagnosing a problem:

  • If one plug fouls (gets coated/wet), the other plug on the same cylinder usually keeps that cylinder running. So a single fouled plug rarely kills a cylinder outright.
  • If a whole cylinder goes dead, the cause is usually both plugs at once — drowned by carb cleaner, running far too rich, or the coil that feeds them is dead. Both plugs share the same combustion chamber, so most causes affect them together.

The "R" matters: the "R" in DPR8EA-9 means the plug has a built-in resistor that suppresses radio noise from the spark. Non-resistor plugs cause electrical interference that confuses the KOSO tach (see tach over-read).

How to access each plug

The two carbs sit between the cylinders, taking up most of the central space. Each cylinder's two plugs are reached from opposite sides of the bike. On a stock Transalp, the fairings hide the front plugs and the tank covers the rear ones. Porikärbes is stripped (no fairings, no shrouds), which makes the front pair easy.

Tools

  • Spark plug socket — the right size lives in the Honda toolkit under the seat (Haynes 1-11 §5.1)
  • Ratchet or T-handle
  • Feeler gauge for the 0.8–0.9 mm electrode gap (the wire-loop type works best on plugs)
  • Compressed air or a clean rag — to blow grit out of the plug well before pulling
  • Torque wrench (optional — 14 Nm is light, hand-feel works fine if you're careful)

Procedure (per plug)

  1. Engine cold. Hot plugs strip threads — the cylinder head is aluminium and easy to damage.
  2. Blow or wipe any grit, leaves or dust out of the well around the plug. Anything that falls into the cylinder when you open it will scratch the bore.
  3. Pull the spark plug cap off straight up. If it's stuck, twist gently while pulling — never yank the lead.
  4. Loosen with the socket. Once it's finger-loose, finish unscrewing by hand to feel for any cross-threading.
  5. Inspect — see "reading a plug" below.
  6. To reinstall: thread the plug in by hand first to avoid cross-threading the soft head. Snug it, then torque to 14 Nm. If you don't have a torque wrench: snug the plug by hand, then ⅛–¼ turn for a re-used plug, ½ turn for a new plug.
  7. Refit the cap. Make sure it clicks fully onto the plug — a loose cap arcs to ground and ruins the lead.

Haynes' rule (page 1-12 §7): "If the electrodes are not excessively worn, if no cracks or chips are visible in the insulator, and if the deposits can be easily removed with a wire brush, the plugs can be re-gapped and re-used. If in doubt concerning the condition of the plugs, replace them with new ones since the expense is minimal." Deposits don't kill plugs; mechanical damage does.

Compare side-to-side. Both plugs in the same cylinder should look similar — they share the chamber. Big differences between the front pair and the rear pair (e.g. front clean, rear sooty) point to a problem with one carb or one coil, not the engine as a whole. Equal sootiness across all four plugs points to a systemic cause (air filter, choke, both pilots set wrong, float level on both bowls).

Idle speed — check and adjustment

Applies to: XL650V Transalp. Separate from mixture tuning — idle speed is the dumb screw that just raises/lowers the throttle plate opening; mixture screws set the fuel-air ratio at idle.

Target

  • 1200 ± 100 rpm when fully warm (Haynes 4-1 / 1-9).
  • Set after valve clearances, carb sync, and mixture are all correct. If idle won't settle, something upstream is wrong — don't chase it with the idle screw.

⚠️ KOSO tach workaround (current Porikärbes-specific)

The KOSO dash currently over-reads RPM by ~1.54×. Until that's resolved (see issue page for pulse=2 test), do not set idle directly to "1200" on the KOSO — you'd actually be at ~780 rpm and the bike will stall.

Target KOSO reading = ~1800 rpm for an actual 1200 rpm. Acceptable range: 1650–1950 on KOSO (= 1100–1300 actual). When the tach issue is fixed, revise this note and use the real 1200 rpm target.

Independent cross-checks (recommended at least once):

  • Handheld inductive tach with clamp-over-plug-wire pickup (~€20–40 on Amazon/AliExpress) — the most reliable DIY check.
  • Timing light with built-in tach mode — many already have this function.
  • By ear (last resort): a warm XL650V V-twin at 1200 rpm sounds steady and relaxed — a slow "lub-lub" cadence. If it sounds busy, racing, or the revs visibly climb with small throttle blips, it's too high. If it hunts or nearly stalls between blips, it's too low.

Where the adjuster is

Knurled plastic knob between the two carbs, reachable from the left side of the bike through the air duct / carb area. Turn by fingers — no tool needed. CW raises idle, CCW lowers it.

Tools

  • Tachometer — KOSO dash is sufficient if calibrated correctly (note: tach currently over-reads, so verify actual RPM with an independent source if in doubt).
  • Stopwatch or ride time for warm-up.

Procedure

  1. Warm the engine fully — 10–15 minutes of stop-and-go riding, or ride until the engine temperature needle reads mid-range.
  2. Park on level ground, centerstand or sidestand with bike upright. Transmission in neutral.
  3. Sweep handlebars full lock-to-lock with the engine idling. If idle changes as you turn: throttle cables are mis-adjusted or worn — fix that first (dangerous — can stick throttle open). See reference for throttle cable freeplay spec (2–6 mm, Haynes 1-2).
  4. Read idle RPM. Target is 1200 ± 100.
  5. Turn the knurled idle adjuster: CW to increase, CCW to decrease. Small increments.
  6. Snap the throttle open and shut 3–4 times. Recheck idle. Adjust again if needed.
  7. Repeat until idle holds at 1200 ± 100 through throttle snaps and bar sweeps.

If idle can't be made steady

Haynes 1-9 §2.5: "If a smooth, steady idle can't be achieved, the fuel/air mixture may be incorrect (see Chapter 4) or the carburettors may need synchronising." Also check:

  • Intake manifold rubbers for cracks (air leak → lean idle)
  • Loose clamps between carbs and head
  • Vacuum hose condition (tap-to-carb, sync takeoffs)
  • Sparking — fouled plugs drop a cylinder at idle

Manual reference

Haynes §2 "Idle speed — check and adjustment" on page 1-9. Component location for the XL650V idle adjuster: page 1-6.

Pilot (mixture) screw tuning

Applies to: XL650V Transalp, both carbs. Keihin CV type, pilot screw per carb, factory spec 3 turns out from lightly seated.

Warning: the stock XL650V has a catalytic converter and Haynes 4-8 warns against running rich because it cooks the cat. Porikärbes has an aftermarket Arrow 72068PD direct-through exhaust with no cat (see parts) — so the cat-cooking risk doesn't apply here. Two reasons to still stay within ±½ turn of factory spec without an exhaust gas analyser: (1) too lean burns valves long-term, and (2) too rich wastes fuel and fouls plugs. Running slightly lean is safer for the engine than slightly rich.

When to tune

  • Idle stalls or hangs high after engine warm-up.
  • Lean-angle stall (one cylinder drops out when leaned) — adjusting the suspect side's pilot screw richer can compensate for a marginal pilot circuit.
  • After carb disassembly/cleaning, to restore a running mixture before final sync.
  • Previous owner's adjustment is suspect (too rich or too lean).

Order of operations (important)

Pilot screws are the last thing to touch. Check/fix in this order:

  1. Valve clearances in spec (Haynes 1-19, spec in Chapter 1).
  2. Air filter clean.
  3. All four spark plugs good — twin-plug head, two per cylinder, NGK DPR8EA-9 with R-type resistor (spark plugs procedure).
  4. Carbs synchronised (Haynes 1-20 §17).
  5. Idle speed at 1200 ± 100 rpm (idle tuning procedure).
  6. Then mixture screws.

Pilot screw locations

Which cylinder is which? The XL650V is a 52° V-twin: the front cylinder leans forward (you see it sticking out ahead of the carbs, exhaust header exits toward the front wheel) and the rear cylinder leans back (tucked under the fuel tank / airbox area). Between them sit the two carbs side-by-side — the forward carb in that pair feeds the front cylinder, the rearward carb feeds the rear cylinder. "Left" and "right" below = rider's left and right when sitting on the bike (same as Haynes LH/RH convention).

The pilot screws are reached from the sides of the bike — no tank removal needed. Porikärbes is stripped (no side fairings, no tank shrouds), so the carbs are fully exposed in the V and access is unobstructed.

  • Front cylinder carb — pilot screw on the RIGHT side of the carb body. Approach from the right side of the bike, screwdriver angled in between frame and carb.
  • Rear cylinder carb — pilot screw on the LEFT side of the carb body. Approach from the left side of the bike, same technique.

On Porikärbes the screw heads sit accessible on the carb body — no deep recess. A standard-length flat-blade screwdriver that fully fills the screw slot is enough; no long slim reach tool needed.

If the screw won't turn (seized)

Symptom: the screw rotates only a fraction of a turn (e.g. ½ turn) before stopping hard in both directions. On 20+ year old Keihin CV carbs this is almost always fuel varnish welding the brass threads into the carb body, often combined with a hardened o-ring. The stops you feel are dried lacquer, not mechanical limits. This is a common failure mode on any carb that has sat with ethanol-era pump fuel for years without the pilot screw being touched — documented across Transalp, Kawasaki KZ, Honda Hawk GT, CX500, and CBX forums.

Do not force past the stops. The classic failure mode is snapping the narrow brass tip off inside the seat. Once that happens, extraction is usually beyond DIY repair (freezer + compressed-air tricks exist but often end in a scrap carb).

Ladder — gentlest first, escalate only if the previous step doesn't free it:

  1. Cycle the slop. Work the screw back and forth within its available range, 20–30 cycles. Rubs solvent into the threads without force. Re-try after each batch.
  2. Penetrating oil soak + tap. A real penetrating oil — designed to creep into corroded threads — is the right tool. The Machinist's Workshop magazine penetrant test (April/May 2007) ranked, best to worst, force needed to break a rusted fit free: 50/50 power steering fluid (or ATF) + acetone (53 lb) > Kroil (106) > Liquid Wrench (127) > PB Blaster (214) > WD-40 (238) > nothing (516). Apply at the base of the screw with a cotton swab or syringe, screw pointing up so fluid sits on the threads. Wait 15+ minutes. Tap the screwdriver handle lightly 5–10 times to shock the threads. Cycle the slop. Try again.
    Hard escalation limit: two soak-cycle-tap rounds (≈30 min total) with no progress = stop in-situ work. Escalate to heat (step 3) or pull the carb (step 5). Repeat-flushing without progress is the failure mode that caused the 2026-04 rear-cylinder-dead incident on this bike — multiple days of carb cleaner ingestion, no movement of the screw, plus likely damage to the rubber seal.
    Do not use carb cleaner or brake cleaner as the soak fluid. Both are aggressive solvents that attack the pilot screw's NBR rubber seal (Haynes 4-13 §21 lists the o-ring as a wear/damage check item). Repeated exposure swells or cracks the seal — and on a twin-plug head like the XL650V you don't get the early warning of a fouled plug, the cylinder just dies. Carb cleaner is for cleaning removed components in a bowl off the bike (Haynes 4-13 §17–19), not in-situ soaking.
  3. Localised heat on the aluminium boss, not the screw. Hair dryer, heat gun on LOW, or hot soldering-iron tip touched against the metal around the screw. Aluminium expands faster than brass and loosens the grip. Short bursts — don't melt nearby rubber. Try turning while warm.
  4. Re-cut the slot BEFORE any real torque. File the screw head flat with a small file, cut a fresh deep slot with a hacksaw blade, use a hollow-ground screwdriver that fully fills the new slot. Cammed-out slots are the most common way a stuck screw becomes a destroyed screw.
  5. Pull the carb. Transalp forum consensus (xrv.org.uk, transalp.org): if heat + overnight ATF/acetone didn't free it, stop working in situ. In-situ work is the single biggest predictor of a snapped tip. Remove the carb, invert it, soak from underneath overnight, work it loose on a bench with a vise.

Do not use:

  • Carb cleaner or brake cleaner as a multi-hour or multi-day soak. Both attack NBR rubber (the pilot screw's seal). Repeated in-situ application swells/cracks the seal. The Cleaning section of Haynes Chapter 4 (page 4-13) endorses petroleum-based solvent only, and only on removed components in a bowl — not in-situ flushing. One brief pass-through during cleaning of disassembled parts is fine; days of in-situ dousing at a stuck screw is what destroys the o-ring (and on a twin-plug head, kills the whole cylinder — see this incident). Use a real penetrating oil instead (step 2).
  • Impact drivers on an undamaged slot — they destroy the slot before shocking the screw loose. Only consider after step 4 above (fresh slot cut).
  • Spiral screw extractors — wedge the screw harder, not looser.
  • WD-40 (original Multi-Use) as primary penetrant — its name stands for Water Displacement, not penetrating oil. WD-40's own marketing emphasises moisture displacement and light lubrication, not deep penetration. The Machinist's Workshop test ranked it slowest of all penetrants tested — barely better than nothing. WD-40 sells a separate "Specialist Penetrant" product that's a different formulation; use that or any of the better options in step 2 instead.

If you end up replacing the screw assembly: pilot screw + spring + washer + o-ring is not a standalone OEM part for the XL650V — it only ships in rebuild kits. Options: Keyster XL650V '00–06 carb kit (nrp-carbs.co.uk), or generic Hippotech pilot-screw kits on Amazon (includes 2× of each part, fits both carbs).

Procedure — record current position first

  1. Position yourself on the right side of the bike for the front carb, left side for the rear carb. Screwdriver angled in between frame and carb body.
  2. Seat the pilot screw — the critical step. Use a flat-blade screwdriver that fills the slot (too narrow = it will cam out and chew the slot). Turn clockwise with the lightest grip you can while keeping the blade engaged. Stop at the first resistance you feel — the moment the screwdriver's rotation stops being free. Do NOT press harder to "confirm." The pilot screw's tip is a fine brass taper that seals on a brass seat in the carb body; forcing past first contact mushrooms the tip. A damaged tip can no longer meter fuel properly and must be replaced (pilot screw + o-ring assembly ~€10–20, or worst case a new carb if the seat is also damaged — hundreds of euros).
  3. Count turns from "free" back to "just seated," including fractions (¼, ½, ¾ visible from the screwdriver handle's rotation). Write down each carb's count separately — these are the PO's settings and your fallback reference.
  4. Back each screw out to its original position (same count as you just recorded). This returns the bike to the PO's state before any of your adjustments.

Procedure — adjustment strategies

Strategy A — factory baseline (fast diagnostic). Good when you suspect the PO set them wrong and want to start clean.

  1. Seat each pilot screw lightly, then back out exactly 3 turns.
  2. Warm the engine fully, set idle to 1200 rpm (idle tuning).
  3. Test for the symptom (lean stall, rough idle, etc.).
  4. If fixed: log both carbs' settings in history.html. Done.
  5. If not fixed: go to Strategy B from the 3-turn starting point.

Strategy B — incremental find-the-peak (per-carb). Finds the highest steady idle on each carb independently.

  1. Engine fully warm, idle set to 1200 rpm.
  2. Work on one carb at a time. Start with the front carb (RH-side screw).
  3. Turn the pilot screw ¼ turn out (richer). Wait 5 seconds for idle to stabilise. Note the RPM.
  4. Turn ¼ turn in from the new position (back to start), then ¼ turn past the start (leaner). Note the RPM.
  5. Set the screw where idle is highest and steadiest.
  6. Reset idle to 1200 rpm (the knob, not the pilot screw).
  7. Repeat on the rear carb (LH-side screw).
  8. Re-verify idle and test the symptom.

Strategy C — symmetric enrich (lean-stall quick fix). When only one cylinder is marginal and you want to enrich both to stay balanced.

  1. From recorded PO baseline, back both screws out an additional ¼ turn.
  2. Reset idle, test.
  3. If improved but not fixed: ¼ more on both (½ turn total from baseline).
  4. No change after ½ turn past baseline on both → stop. Problem is not pilot screw position — pilot circuit is likely clogged or float height is wrong. Proceed to carb overhaul (see service procedure).

After adjustment

  • Reset idle speed if enrichening raised it.
  • Snap throttle 3–4 times, recheck idle stability.
  • Test ride — check cold start, warm idle, and the original symptom.
  • Log final turn-out value for each carb separately in history.html. These values matter for future work.

Troubleshooting

  • No change across the full ¼-½-¾ turn sweep: pilot circuit is blocked downstream of the screw. Carb needs cleaning (full service).
  • Idle rises but then hunts: air leak at intake rubber or vacuum hose. Fix the leak first.
  • One carb responds, the other doesn't: the unresponsive side's pilot jet or passage is blocked. Pull that carb.
  • Screw feels gritty or won't turn smoothly: tip is damaged or o-ring is swollen. Pilot screw assembly: screw + spring + washer + o-ring (Haynes 4-15 §10.3). Replace the o-ring at minimum.
  • Symptom is one cylinder running rich + sooty plugs: a breached o-ring lets fuel leak past the screw threads outward into the venturi (rich) — like an open tap. Distinct from the lean-leak case (air leaking in past the seal). Both are o-ring failures; the breach side determines which way the leak goes. CBR250 forum precedent shows the rich variant: missing o-ring → fouled plug → dropped cylinder. Fix is the same: replace the o-ring (whole assembly preferred).

Ignition coil — basic test (multimeter)

Applies to: XL650V Transalp.

What this tests, and why it matters here

The XL650V has one ignition coil per cylinder, each one driving both plugs on that cylinder via a Y-shaped HT lead (Haynes 5-2 §1). If a coil dies, BOTH plugs on its cylinder lose spark and the whole cylinder goes dead — the same symptom as carb-cleaner drowning, but with a totally different fix. This procedure tells you which it is, in five minutes, without removing anything.

Tools

  • Cheap digital multimeter, set to the resistance (Ω) range

Reading the result

  • All numbers in spec: coil is electrically healthy. The dead-cylinder cause is somewhere else.
  • Primary reads 0 Ω, or open / "OL" / "1.": primary winding shorted or broken. Coil dead — replace.
  • Secondary very high or open: secondary winding broken. Coil dead — replace.
  • Caps in spec but secondary-without-caps reads low or 0: internal short between primary and secondary. Coil dead — replace.
  • Coil readings OK but cap readings out of spec: just replace the suspect cap (it unscrews off the HT lead).

Where the coils are mounted

  • Front coil: on the frame near the front cylinder. Easiest from above with the tank propped up (Haynes 5-4 photo 3.18b shows the location on an XL650V).
  • Rear coil: near the rear cylinder, also tank-up access (Haynes 5-4 photo 3.18c).

Quick alternative — visual spark check

If you don't have a multimeter, you can confirm a coil is firing by looking at the spark itself:

  1. Pull a plug, leave it connected to its cap and HT lead.
  2. Lay the threaded body of the plug against any clean bare metal on the engine (a head bolt, a fin) so it's grounded.
  3. Crank the engine on the starter for 1–2 seconds with the kill switch in the run position.
  4. You should see a fat, blue, snappy spark jump the gap. Thin yellow spark, intermittent spark, or no spark = coil, cap or lead problem.

Safety: don't hold the plug or HT lead with bare hands during the test — coil voltage is in the tens of thousands of volts. Use insulated pliers, or wrap a thick rag around the plug.
